# Signal Taxonomy

The catalog of signals used to reason about authorship. This is reference material, not a checklist to tally. Signals vary enormously in reliability; a few strong ones outweigh many weak ones, and almost all of them have innocent explanations that must be ruled out before they count. **Read the anti-signals section at the end before finalizing any assessment.**

Each signal carries a reliability rating:

- **Strong** — hard to produce innocently; meaningful even alone, though still confirmed against context.
- **Moderate** — real evidence in a cluster; weak in isolation.
- **Weak / noisy** — only meaningful when several co-occur and innocent explanations are excluded. High false-positive risk.

## 1. Linguistic signals (AI-leaning)

**Reflexive both-sides hedging.** *(Moderate–Strong)* Compulsive balancing where a human would commit: “While X has benefits, it also presents challenges.” “It’s important to consider both sides.” The tell is that the balance appears *regardless of whether the topic warrants it* — even trivial claims get hedged. Humans hedge selectively, where they actually feel the tension.

**Throat-clearing meta-commentary.** *(Moderate)* Phrases that announce the writing instead of doing it: “It’s worth noting that…”, “It’s important to understand that…”, “One key aspect to consider is…”, “In this section, we will explore…”. A human writer usually just says the thing.

**Hollow intensifiers and abstraction.** *(Moderate)* “rapidly evolving landscape,” “ever-changing world,” “fast-paced environment,” “in today’s digital age,” “plays a crucial/vital/pivotal role,” “a powerful tool.” Content-free scaffolding that sounds substantive and says nothing.

**Concept-noun stacking.** *(Moderate)* Sentences built from abstractions with no concrete referent: “leveraging synergies to drive holistic, scalable engagement.” The prose can be grammatically perfect and still refer to nothing you could point at.

**Uniform transition habit.** *(Moderate)* Mechanical sentence/paragraph openers: “Furthermore,” “Moreover,” “Additionally,” “In conclusion,” “Ultimately,” used as connective reflex rather than to mark a real logical turn. Watch for the same handful recurring on a regular beat.

**Absence of contractions in casual register.** *(Weak)* Formal “do not / cannot / it is” in a context where a person would naturally write “don’t / can’t / it’s.” Weak because plenty of humans write formally and some genres require it — only counts when the register is otherwise casual.

**Over-explanation of the obvious.** *(Moderate)* Defining terms the audience plainly knows, restating the premise before every point, closing loops no reader needed closed. AI optimizes for a generic reader who knows nothing; humans write for the reader they actually have.

**Suspiciously even rhythm.** *(Weak–Moderate)* Sentence lengths clustering in a narrow band, few very short or very long sentences, little fragment-or-sprawl variation. Genuine human prose tends to have more burstiness — a one-word sentence next to a forty-word one. Weak alone; meaningful with other signals. (Note: heavily edited human prose can smooth this out, and some AI deliberately varies it now.)

**Vocabulary that’s elevated but not idiosyncratic.** *(Weak)* A reach for the slightly fancier word (“utilize,” “facilitate,” “myriad,” “delve,” “tapestry,” “realm”) without the personal verbal fingerprints a specific human carries. The presence of “delve” is near-meaningless on its own and badly overused as a tell — only note the *pattern* of consistently elevated, impersonal diction.

**Clean logical march with no detours.** *(Moderate)* Every paragraph advances the thesis; nothing wanders, doubles back, or gets distracted by something interesting-but-tangential. Human thinking leaves tracks: asides, reconsiderations, a point made then qualified two paragraphs later.

## 2. Structural signals (AI-leaning)

**List reflex.** *(Moderate)* Defaulting to bullets and numbered lists for material that prose would carry better, especially lists with parallel-structured items each opening with a bolded lead-in. Enterprise content does use lists legitimately — the signal is the *reflex*, lists where they don’t earn their place.

**Header saturation.** *(Moderate)* A heading every two or three paragraphs, sub-sections for short stretches of text, structure imposed beyond what the content’s complexity requires.

**Paragraph uniformity.** *(Moderate–Strong)* Paragraphs of near-identical length and identical internal shape (topic sentence → two support sentences → transition), repeated down the page. This architectural regularity is one of the more reliable structural tells — humans produce lumpier paragraphs.

**The rule-of-three reflex.** *(Weak–Moderate)* Everything arrives in threes: three examples, three benefits, three adjectives (“clear, concise, and compelling”). Humans use triads too, but the *relentless* default to three is a generation habit.

**Telegraphed scaffolding.** *(Moderate)* Explicit “Introduction / Body / Conclusion” logic surfaced in the text — “First, we’ll examine… Next… Finally…” then a “In conclusion” that restates the opening. Formulaic intro-restates-thesis and conclusion-restates-intro bookending.

**Symmetric sections.** *(Weak)* Each section roughly the same weight and depth, as if filling a template, rather than depth tracking where the real substance is.

## 3. Narrative / content signals

These are often the **strongest** signals, because specificity and stance are the hardest things for AI to fake and the easiest for a human to supply.

**Absence of real-world specificity.** *(Strong)* No named people, places, dates, products, numbers, or incidents — or only generic placeholders (“a leading company,” “studies show,” “many experts believe,” “a recent report”). Plausible-but-unverifiable detail is a hallmark of generation. *Concrete, checkable, idiosyncratic specifics* are the single best evidence of a human who was actually there.

**No stakes, no cost.** *(Strong)* The writer never reveals what they have riding on the argument, what they got wrong, what it cost them, what they’re unsure of in a way that matters to them. AI argues from nowhere; humans argue from somewhere.

**Manufactured-sounding anecdotes.** *(Moderate–Strong)* Stories that are structurally perfect and emotionally generic — “Imagine a small business owner named Sarah who…” The anecdote serves the point too cleanly and contains no surprising, pointless, true-feeling detail. Real memory is messy and includes things that don’t serve the argument.

**Stance-flatness.** *(Moderate)* No real opinion held under pressure. Everything is “can be beneficial” / “may pose challenges.” A human with expertise usually has a take, and reveals it, and sometimes a slightly unreasonable one.

**Genre-perfect blandness.** *(Moderate)* The piece hits every convention of its genre and risks nothing — no joke that might not land, no structural experiment, no sentence the author clearly loved. Competence without personality.

**Knowledge that’s broad but not deep.** *(Moderate)* Confident coverage of the standard talking points with no insider detail, no “the thing nobody tells you,” no correction of a common misconception from lived experience. AI knows what’s written about a topic; practitioners know what isn’t.

**Citations that don’t resolve.** *(Strong, when checkable)* References to studies, quotes, or sources that are vague, slightly wrong, or non-existent. Confabulated specifics. If a named source can be checked and is fabricated or garbled, that is strong evidence.

## 4. Human-positive signals (evidence *of* a person)

The absence of AI signals is not the presence of a human. Look affirmatively for these — they push toward “human” and complicate any AI read:

- **Idiosyncratic voice** — a recurring verbal habit, a personal cadence, words used in a slightly-off personal way.
- **Earned specificity** — the offhand precise detail that serves no argumentative purpose and is therefore probably true (the brand of coffee, the exact wrong thing someone said in 2019).
- **Productive imperfection** — a sentence fragment for emphasis, a tangent the writer chases because it’s interesting, an asymmetry in structure, a deliberately unbalanced argument.
- **Opinion with skin in it** — a claim the writer is clearly willing to be wrong about and own.
- **Real intertextuality** — references to specific things the writer has actually read/seen/done, deployed naturally rather than as decoration.
- **Humor that risks not landing**, profanity in register, genuine surprise, a changed mind mid-piece.
- **Local error** — a typo, a small continuity slip, an inconsistency of the kind clean generation rarely produces (though good human editing removes these too, so absence is not an AI tell).

## 5. Anti-signals — what is NOT evidence (false-positive guard)

**Run every candidate AI signal through this before counting it.** These are the patterns that produce wrongful flags. Treating them as tells is how real detectors harm real people.

- **The em-dash is not an AI tell.** Skilled human writers love em-dashes. So does AI. It carries almost no signal. The same goes for the Oxford comma, the semicolon, and “moreover” in isolation.
- **A single buzzword is not evidence.** “Delve,” “tapestry,” “leverage,” “robust,” “navigate the landscape” — one or two of these means nothing. Humans use them too, especially in corporate registers. Only a *dense convergence* counts.
- **Formal, hedged, structured prose is mandatory in some genres.** Legal writing, academic papers, regulatory filings, technical documentation, and corporate comms *require* the very features that look AI-ish. In these genres, formality is null evidence; look instead at specificity, stance, and whether the structure exceeds even genre norms.
- **ESL and non-native English raise false-positive risk sharply.** Non-native writers often produce formal register, limited contractions, careful structure, and slightly elevated vocabulary — none of which indicates AI. This is one of the most important guards. When ESL is plausible, weight surface-linguistic signals far lower and lean on narrative signals instead.
- **A strong house style mimics AI uniformity.** Heavily edited corporate or brand content is smoothed toward exactly the regularity AI produces. Editing is not generation.
- **Cleanliness is not a tell.** Good writers and good editors produce error-free, well-structured prose. Polish ≠ AI.
- **AI has moved on from the obvious tells.** Current models can be prompted to vary rhythm, use contractions, drop the throat-clearing, and inject fake specificity. Absence of the classic tells does *not* clear a piece. This cuts both ways: it raises false-negative risk and means high confidence requires affirmative human signal, not just absent AI signal.
- **Short text can’t carry a confident read.** Under ~150 words, decline to be confident.

## 6. How to weigh it all

1. **Cluster, don’t tally.** The question is whether signals *converge across categories* (linguistic + structural + narrative all pointing one way), not how long the list is.
1. **Strong narrative signals dominate.** Real, checkable specificity and genuine stakes are the best human evidence; their total absence plus plausible confabulation is the best AI evidence. Surface-linguistic signals are supporting evidence, rarely decisive.
1. **Exclude the innocent explanation first.** For each AI-leaning signal, ask: genre requirement? ESL? house style? If yes, downgrade.
1. **Affirmative human signal is required for “Likely human,” not just absent AI signal** — because modern AI can suppress its tells.
1. **Localize for hybrid.** Most enterprise content is hybrid. Find the seam: human fingerprints in the anecdotes and specifics, machine residue in the connective tissue, transitions, and summaries.
1. **Calibrate confidence to evidence, and report what would change it.** Thin or conflicting evidence → Low confidence or “Insufficient signal.” Say what additional information (author context, a longer sample, source-checking) would move the read.
