# Humanization Playbook

For **author mode**. The user owns the piece and wants it to read as credibly human. This is the catalog of transformations that remove machine residue without flattening the author’s voice.

## The one rule that governs all the others

**Humanizing is not “applying good-writing rules.” It is restoring specificity, stance, and stakes — the three things AI structurally cannot supply and a human can.** You can fix every surface tell and still have an obviously synthetic piece if it refers to nothing real and risks nothing. Conversely, a piece thick with concrete detail and a genuine point of view reads human even with imperfect prose. **Lead the guidance with the substance fixes, not the cosmetic ones.**

And: **protect the voice.** The failure mode of humanization is homogenizing toward a generic competent style. Name the passages already working and tell the author to keep them. The target is *their* voice with the machine residue removed, not a house style.

## Priority order

When briefing an author, lead with the highest-leverage moves and stop before the list gets exhaustive:

1. **Inject real specificity** (highest leverage — fixes the strongest AI signal)
1. **Surface a stance and stakes**
1. **Cut throat-clearing and hollow abstraction**
1. **Break structural uniformity**
1. **Fix rhythm and connective tissue** (lowest leverage — cosmetic; do last)

Show before/after on the author’s own worst offenders rather than reciting rules.

## Pattern 1 — Inject real specificity

The fix that matters most. Replace generic, plausible, unverifiable content with concrete, checkable, idiosyncratic detail.

- Named placeholders → real names, products, dates, numbers, places.
- “Studies show,” “experts agree,” “research suggests” → the actual study, the actual number, or cut the claim.
- “Imagine a business owner who…” → a specific thing that specifically happened.
- Generic example → the offhand precise detail that’s true and slightly pointless (these are the most human thing on the page).

*Before:* “Many organizations have found that adopting AI tools can significantly improve productivity across various functions.”
*After:* “When we put Claude into the RFP intake step, the first-draft turnaround went from three days to about four hours. The legal team still hated it, which is its own data point.”

If the author can’t supply real specifics, that’s the real problem — the piece may be arguing about something they don’t have ground truth on. Say so.

## Pattern 2 — Surface a stance and stakes

AI argues from nowhere. Give the piece a somewhere.

- Find the “can be beneficial / may pose challenges” both-sides sentences and make them commit, *or* name the real tension the author actually feels (not a manufactured symmetry).
- Add what the author has riding on this: what they got wrong, what it cost, what they’re still unsure about, what they’d defend under pushback.
- Let one opinion be a little unreasonable. Expertise shows up as a take, not as balance.

*Before:* “There are advantages and disadvantages to remote work, and organizations must weigh these carefully.”
*After:* “I ran a distributed team for six years and I’ll say the thing nobody says: most ‘remote doesn’t work’ complaints are management failures wearing a costume.”

## Pattern 3 — Cut throat-clearing and hollow abstraction

Delete the meta-commentary and the content-free scaffolding. This is mostly subtraction.

- “It’s important to note that X” → “X.”
- “In today’s rapidly evolving landscape…” → cut, or name the actual moment.
- “plays a crucial role in,” “is a powerful tool for,” “serves as a key driver of” → say what it actually does.
- Concept-noun stacks (“leveraging synergies to drive holistic engagement”) → name the concrete thing, or cut.

The test for any sentence: does it carry information, or does it announce that information is coming? Cut the announcers.

## Pattern 4 — Break structural uniformity

Make the architecture track the content instead of a template.

- Dissolve reflexive lists back into prose where prose carries it better. Keep lists only where parallel items genuinely warrant them.
- Remove headers the content doesn’t need; let sections run to the length their substance demands rather than matching each other.
- Kill the telegraphed scaffolding: no “First… Next… Finally,” no conclusion that restates the intro.
- Let paragraph lengths get lumpy. A one-line paragraph is allowed. So is a long one.
- Break the rule-of-three reflex — sometimes two, sometimes five, sometimes one.

## Pattern 5 — Fix rhythm and connective tissue (do this last)

Cosmetic, but it removes the residual hum.

- Vary sentence length deliberately. Put a short, blunt sentence next to a long one. Fragments are fine for emphasis.
- Replace mechanical transitions (“Furthermore,” “Moreover,” “Additionally”) with either a real logical connector or nothing — often the sentences just butt against each other and it reads better.
- Allow contractions where the register is conversational.
- Let a little roughness stand. A perfectly smooth sentence is sometimes less trustworthy than a slightly awkward true one.

-----

## What to leave alone

- Passages with real specificity, a clear voice, or genuine stakes — protect these explicitly; they’re the human core.
- Formality the genre actually requires (legal, regulatory, technical). Don’t “humanize” a contract into casualness.
- The author’s idiosyncrasies, even slightly awkward ones — those *are* the human signal. Don’t sand them off in the name of polish.

## A caution on the goal

There’s a difference between *making AI-assisted writing genuinely more human* (adding real substance, voice, stakes) and *disguising AI authorship to defeat detection*. The first is editing and it’s legitimate. The second, in contexts where provenance has been asked for or disclosure is owed (academic, journalistic, regulatory, contractual), is deception. If the request is plainly about evading a disclosure obligation rather than improving the work, name that distinction plainly rather than just executing. In ordinary professional and marketing contexts, improving authenticity is just good editing — proceed.
