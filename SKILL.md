# AI Authorship Analysis

A signal-based reasoning system for assessing whether written content was authored by a human, generated by AI, or produced through human–AI collaboration — and for guiding the work of making AI-assisted content read as credibly human.

## What this skill is — and what it refuses to be

This is **not a detector**. It does not output a probability score, a percentage, or a verdict that could survive being treated as ground truth. Real AI-detection tools that do this are unreliable, and their false positives have material consequences: skilled writers, non-native English speakers, and people writing in constrained genres get flagged for the wrong reasons.

This skill does something more defensible and more useful: it **reads the way an expert editor reads**, names the specific signals it sees, reasons about what those signals are evidence *of*, weighs the evidence honestly, and produces an **explainable assessment** with calibrated confidence. The reasoning is the product. A classification with no visible evidence is a failure of this skill, not a success.

Core commitments:

- **Evidence over verdict.** Every classification is backed by named, quoted signals.
- **Clusters over tells.** No single feature decides anything. An em-dash is not an AI tell. A *pattern* of features, converging, is evidence.
- **Calibrated humility.** When the evidence is thin or conflicting, say so. “Insufficient signal to distinguish” is a valid and often correct output.
- **Guard against false positives.** Actively check whether a signal has an innocent explanation before counting it (see the anti-signals section in the taxonomy).

## The two modes

The skill operates in one of two modes. Infer the mode from how the request is framed; when ambiguous, ask one short question.

**Reviewer mode (transparency & audit).** The user wants to know the provenance of a piece someone *else* may have written or AI-assisted. The deliverable is an assessment: classification, confidence, the evidence behind it, and — where relevant — which sections look human vs. machine. Register is neutral and forensic. Do not editorialize about quality unless asked.

**Author mode (quality uplift).** The user owns the piece and wants it to read as credibly human — better tone, more authentic voice, higher trust. The deliverable is the assessment *plus* prioritized, specific humanization guidance. Register is collaborative and direct. Protect the author’s voice; the goal is to remove machine residue, not to impose a house style.

|-------------------|----------------------|----------------------------------|
|Whose content      |Someone else’s (audit)|The user’s own                    |
|Primary deliverable|Explainable assessment|Assessment + humanization guidance|

## The classifications

- **Likely human-written** — Signals of authentic authorship dominate; few or no AI artifacts, and those present have plausible innocent explanations.
- **Likely AI-generated** — A converging cluster of AI artifacts across multiple signal categories, with little evidence of human-specific authorship.
- **Hybrid (AI-assisted)** — The realistic default for most enterprise, marketing, and professional content. Evidence of both: human specificity and stakes in some passages, machine connective tissue and structure in others. Where possible, localize *which* parts read which way.

When evidence genuinely doesn’t separate the cases, the honest output is **“Insufficient signal.”** Do not force a classification to seem decisive.

## Workflow

### 1. Frame the analysis

Determine mode (reviewer vs. author). Note anything that shifts base rates and false-positive risk *before* reading for signals:

- **Genre and constraints.** Legal, academic, regulatory, and technical-doc genres mandate formal, hedged, structured prose — features that look “AI-ish” but are required by the form. Marketing copy has its own conventions. Adjust expectations accordingly.
- **Author context.** Non-native English, a strong house style, ESL-influenced syntax, or a known formal writer all raise false-positive risk. Flag this if known; don’t assume it if unknown.
- **Length.** Short texts (under ~150 words) rarely carry enough signal for a confident read. Say so rather than overreaching.

### 2. Scan for signals

Read the full text and inventory signals across the three categories. **Read `references/signal-taxonomy.md` for the full catalog** — what each signal is, why it correlates with AI or human authorship, and how reliable it is.

- **Linguistic** — diction, register, rhythm, hedging, transition habits, contractions, idiom.
- **Structural** — organization, formatting density, paragraph uniformity, signposting, list reflexes.
- **Narrative / content** — specificity, stance, stakes, real proper nouns, lived detail, voice, the willingness to be uneven.

For each notable signal, note the direction (AI-leaning / human-leaning), strength, and a short verbatim example from the text. **Crucially, run each candidate AI signal through the anti-signals check** in the taxonomy — does it have an innocent explanation (genre, ESL, house style)? If yes, downgrade or discard it.

### 3. Reason to a classification

Step back from the inventory and weigh it. The question is not “how many AI signals are there” but “does a *converging cluster* across categories point one way, and does the alternative explanation hold up?”

- A pile of weak surface signals with innocent explanations ≠ AI.
- A few strong, hard-to-fake signals (e.g., uniform paragraph architecture + reflexive both-sides hedging + zero real-world specificity, all at once) ≫ a long list of weak ones.
- For hybrid, identify the seam: which passages carry human fingerprints, which read as generated.

### 4. Produce the output

**Reviewer mode output:**

1. **Classification** — one of the four, stated plainly.
1. **Confidence** — High / Moderate / Low, with one sentence on *why* (what would raise or lower it).
1. **Key evidence** — the 3–6 strongest signals, each named, with a verbatim snippet and a one-line read. Lead with the strongest. Group by category if there are many.
1. **Counter-evidence / caveats** — signals pointing the other way, innocent explanations considered, base-rate notes. This section is mandatory; an assessment with no counter-evidence section is not finished.
1. **(If hybrid) Section map** — which parts read human, which machine.

**Author mode output:** everything above, plus:
6. **Humanization guidance** — prioritized, specific, voice-preserving. **Read `references/humanization-playbook.md`** for the artifact-by-artifact transformation patterns. Lead with the 2–3 highest-impact interventions, not an exhaustive list. Show before/after on the worst offenders using the author’s own text. Name what to *keep* — protect the passages already working. The goal is removing machine residue, never homogenizing toward a generic “good writing” voice.

## Format discipline

This skill produces *reasoning*, not a dashboard. Write the assessment as an editor would brief a colleague: prose with targeted structure where it earns its place. Quote the text. Avoid turning the output into a wall of bullets or a scorecard — that performs rigor instead of demonstrating it. The single most important quality bar: **a reader should finish the assessment understanding not just the verdict but exactly what in the text drove it, and where the analysis could be wrong.**
